From Dean — directly to Addenbrooke's

I blagged my way into your detox.
The next person shouldn't have to.

I negotiated my way into Addenbrooke's for a final detox because I had no clean route in. I wasn't officially eligible in the way the system expected. But I knew it was my best chance, so I found a way in. It worked — but it shouldn't require that. The next person in that situation may not have the words, the energy or the luck.

What OTOS does at the Addenbrooke's touchpoint is simple: a bedside moment before discharge. Mobile-first. Logged in seconds. A handoff receipt issued. An onward route selected. The person leaves the ward with a receipted next step — not a verbal promise that vanishes the moment they hit the car park.

No EPR write-back. No new clinical record. No diagnosis. Just the handoff, logged and visible to the partner team until it is confirmed as landed.

"The failure was not inside the ward. It was the moment between leaving Addenbrooke's and arriving somewhere else. That gap is exactly what OTOS is designed to hold."
What the bedside moment looks like
  • →Register touchpoint — logged in under 60 seconds
  • →Select onward route — CGL, CPFT, GP, RCE
  • →Handoff receipt issued — visible to both sides
  • →Acknowledgement signal — logged when received
  • →Drift detected — human review triggered if silence follows
Clear boundaries

What OTOS will never do.
And what stays entirely with you.

No new clinical record

OTOS logs a continuity signal — not a clinical event. No new record is created. Nothing writes back to any EPR system. The clinical record stays exactly where it is.

No diagnosis or prescribing

OTOS does not diagnose, prescribe, recommend treatment or make any clinical decision. Every action in the system requires human review and human decision.

Clinical leadership stays explicit

Where eligibility criteria are met under clinical leadership and local governance, the continuity layer carries the signal — while the clinical team stays in control of every decision.
