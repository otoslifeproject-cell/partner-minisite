01
The Problem

A significant cohort of adults in Cambridge are cycling through addiction services, mental health crisis care, revolving-door hospitalisation and homelessness — while the underlying driver of their behaviour remains undiagnosed and untreated.

Research from the Royal College of Psychiatrists and NICE consistently shows that ADHD is present in 23–45% of adults in addiction treatment — and that treating the underlying ADHD produces measurable improvements in both ADHD symptoms and substance use outcomes.

Current system problem: These individuals are known to multiple services — but each service sees only its part of the picture. No one holds the thread. No one connects the dots. The result is fragmented, reactive, and expensive care that does not address the root cause.

02
The OTOS Continuity Solution

OTOS Continuity creates a connecting layer that links care, support and recovery services around each person's real journey. It does not replace existing services — it connects them into one coordinated pathway.

  • Identify — screen individuals in addiction and recovery services for likely undiagnosed ADHD using a validated pathway
  • Connect — warm-transfer to appropriate NHS and partner services: assessment, Right to Choose, GP, CPFT, mental health
  • Coordinate — maintain a continuity thread so each partner knows where the person is in their journey
  • Sustain — support appropriate medication, ongoing recovery, and re-engagement — building evidence for a permanent model
03
Evidence Base
Key Supporting Evidence
RCPsych 2022
"There is more benefit than risk from treating ADHD in patients with substance use disorder — for both ADHD and substance use symptom reduction."
NICE NG87
NICE guidance supports assessment and treatment of ADHD even in active substance use contexts, and covers ADHD with coexisting substance misuse specifically.
Progress in Mind 2022
"Lisdexamfetamine (Elvanse) appeared to reduce craving in cocaine misuse. No evidence of misuse was found in patients receiving properly supervised treatment."
NCBI 2024
"People with ADHD have a significantly raised risk of drug and alcohol misuse, which further complicates ADHD diagnosis and management in standard clinical settings."
CGL Nottingham 2024–25
Change Grow Live — a core OTOS partner — trialled an ADHD identification and referral pathway in Nottingham. Frontline addiction workers, with light-touch training, successfully screened existing caseloads for likely ADHD. Warm referral into assessment produced significantly better engagement than standard routes. The groundwork for this model is already proven in a comparable setting.

06
What We Are Asking For
  • Public Health and ICB engagement and support for the pre-pilot demonstrator — recognising the cost-saving potential within existing drug and alcohol budgets
  • Governance alignment — connecting this to existing NHS commissioning, NHS Right to Choose pathways and Local Authority drug and alcohol commissioning frameworks
  • Budget intelligence — guidance on where existing spend could be redirected, and which funding streams are most relevant to this model
  • Partner introductions — to ICB commissioning leads, NHS teams, and VCSE partners not yet in the ecosystem
  • A route toward formal commissioning once the demonstrator builds the evidence base — creating a permanent, funded, joined-up pathway

A note on lived experience: OTOS Continuity was developed by someone with direct personal experience of this pathway — who went through addiction, crisis, and finally received an ADHD diagnosis and was prescribed Elvanse privately. The transformation was immediate. The model is not theoretical. It is built from lived understanding of where the system fails — and what joined-up, properly supported care can achieve.
