The Public Health angle

This is not a new cost.
It is a smarter use of what the system already spends.

Drug and alcohol commissioning budgets are already under pressure. But a significant portion of current spend is reactive — managing crisis presentations, repeat engagement, revolving-door hospitalisation, housing pressure, custody contact and people who never address the root cause of their addiction.

OTOS proposes a different model: identify and treat the underlying driver earlier, hold the person through the transition, and make continuity visible enough to test whether reactive demand reduces. Not through cuts. Through better-connected, earlier, smarter intervention.

Current spend pattern
Reactive and fragmented. A&E presentations, crisis mental health, repeat addiction treatment episodes, police and custody involvement, housing and homelessness support. Each service sees only its piece — and bills accordingly.
With OTOS Continuity
Connected and preventive. One identification. One coordinated pathway. ADHD assessment where appropriate, medication route where clinically indicated, supported recovery, fewer crisis episodes, and visible follow-through across budget lines.
The system is already paying. The question is whether it is paying early enough, visibly enough, and in the right place.
Discharge risk

Closed is not always safe.
Sometimes the risk has just moved out of view.

A system can appear to succeed when a person is discharged, closed, referred on, or marked as having completed an episode. But if nobody can see whether the next step actually happened, the risk has not disappeared — it has simply moved out of view.

OTOS helps distinguish genuine progress from invisible drift by making handoffs receipted, follow-through visible, and silence reviewable before it becomes another crisis presentation.

Episode closed
Current systems can record closure without confirming whether the next service actually received the person.
Handoff receipted
OTOS records whether the expected next step was acknowledged, landed, stalled or went quiet.
Risk visible
Silence becomes a reviewable signal, not a hidden re-presentation waiting to happen.

The model

Continuity infrastructure
with message-delivery and micro-check-in capability.

Partners can approve short, familiar messages for their own touchpoints — for example a CGL welcome message, an RCE course nudge while someone waits, or a discharge follow-through confirmation. OTOS provides the infrastructure; the partner decides the wording, timing and responsibility.

The micro-check-in layer is deliberately simple: one low-friction question, repeated at agreed points, giving a basic continuity signal without requiring a keyworker to pick up the phone every time. It is not clinical monitoring and it does not replace human care.

The point is not to watch people. The point is to keep the pathway warm enough that people do not disappear unnoticed.

Public value test

Not technology theatre.
Continuity infrastructure.

“Is this just an opportunity for tech firms to make money out of the taxpayer?”

It is a legitimate question. The honest answer is: only if OTOS is framed as technology theatre rather than continuity infrastructure.

OTOS is not individual-level behavioural monitoring. It is cross-agency continuity infrastructure — designed to change whether the handoff happens, whether services can see what happened next, and whether silent drift becomes visible before crisis.

The public value test
Does it change whether people reach the next service after a handoff?

Does it make silent disengagement visible earlier?

Does it reduce avoidable re-presentation and crisis cost?

Can it be governed, tested and improved with the data it generates?
Why a pre-pilot matters
If yes, it is worth the investment. If no, the pre-pilot shows that clearly — at £91,520 rather than millions. The point is to test before scale, not commit before evidence.
