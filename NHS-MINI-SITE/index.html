The problem

Hidden in plain sight.
ADHD is driving addiction.

Thousands of people are cycling through addiction services, mental health crisis, homelessness and revolving-door hospitalisation — while the underlying cause remains undiagnosed and untreated.

The system usually treats addiction as the primary condition. For some people, that is right. For a significant and identifiable group of adults, it is not. The primary condition is ADHD. The drinking or drug use is what arrives in view.

At that point, services quite reasonably respond to the addiction. But if the ADHD remains untouched, the work does not hold. CBT is delivered to a brain that cannot sequence or retain it. Motivation strategies meet an executive function that was never the problem.

23–45%

Of people in addiction treatment have undiagnosed ADHD

Royal College of Psychiatrists research shows ADHD is overrepresented in addiction cohorts and consistently missed at point of entry.

Much of this spend is reactive. Treating the symptom without identifying the underlying neurodevelopmental cause keeps people trapped in cycles.

What OTOS creates

One safe continuity signal.
Not another service to manage.

OTOS does not replace existing services. It gives them a shared, governed continuity layer: one anonymised journey token, one visible handoff trail, one way to see whether the next step actually landed.

What it helps partners achieve

  • Handoffs land more visibly because the next step can be receipted, acknowledged or marked as stalled.
  • Silence becomes visible earlier so drift can be reviewed before it becomes crisis, relapse or re-presentation.
  • Waiting-list time is less blind because the person can still have a lightweight continuity thread while services move.
  • Frontline effort is protected because a touchpoint can be logged quickly, without starting a new clinical record.
  • Governance improves because commissioners can see a timestamped audit trail of what happened between services.

What stays protected

  • Clinical notes stay where they are. OTOS is not an EPR, diagnostic platform or shared clinical record.
  • Clinical decisions stay with partners. OTOS surfaces signals; human teams decide what happens next.
  • Full record-sharing is not required by default. The continuity signal can be tokenised and anonymous.
  • Safeguarding and crisis protocols remain unchanged. OTOS does not replace partner responsibilities.
  • No service starts again from scratch. OTOS sits around the work already happening and makes the gaps visible.
How it works

Four steps.
One connected pathway.

The person can remain anonymous. The clinical notes stay where they are. The continuity signal travels.

1

Identify

Screen and identify people in addiction, recovery, crisis or waiting-list pathways who show indicators of co-occurring ADHD and substance misuse. OTOS does not diagnose — it surfaces the signal.

2

Connect

Warm-transfer people to appropriate NHS and partner routes — ADHD assessment, Right to Choose, CPFT, GP, mental health support or recovery support. The receipt is logged. The handoff becomes visible.

3

Co-ordinate

Maintain a continuity thread across services so each partner knows where the person is in the journey — reducing gaps, duplication and silent disengagement before it becomes crisis.

4

Sustain

Support appropriate follow-through, ongoing recovery and re-engagement — building locally validated evidence for a permanent joined-up pathway.

Micro-check-ins

A tiny signal can change the timing.
Before anyone has to chase.

Micro-check-ins are not therapy and not monitoring. They are simple, agreed “sign of life” prompts — one question, one response, one lightweight continuity signal.

Less blind waiting

A person on a list can still create a safe signal that they are alive, engaged and reachable without a keyworker having to pick up the phone every time.

Better-timed support

If the signal drops, partners can see it earlier and decide whether a human review or safe re-engagement route is needed.

ADHD-informed repetition

Low-friction repetition, habit stacking and simple prompts can help maintain engagement while formal treatment routes move.
