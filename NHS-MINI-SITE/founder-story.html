Phase 1
The Journey — Self / Undiagnosed

For much of his adult life, Dean Butler's drinking appeared to work. He was a DJ, a promoter, part of the Cambridge scene. Alcohol gave energy, focus, relief from mental friction. He was high-functioning enough that the question nobody ever asked was: why does this work so well? The answer, when it finally came, changed everything.

    Pancreatic attack — "You can never drink again"
    Hospital admission in Spain. Pancreatic attack. Spanish doctor delivers a clear, honest verdict: "You can never drink again." First time this was said clearly by a clinician. Dean felt relief, not fear. The threat of death felt like permission to stop.
  • UK
    GP (UK)
    "You're healthy enough to drink"
    Back in the UK, Dean sees a GP who tells him he is healthy enough and effectively gives permission to continue drinking. Direct contradiction of the Spanish doctor's clear clinical advice. No continuity of care has transferred. The thread broke the moment he crossed the Channel.

The system has no thread to hold.

Three countries. Multiple GPs. Two contradictory clinical verdicts. One diagnosis that exists on paper but has no pathway attached. The ADHD is identified but not treated in the context of the addiction. Every service sees only what arrives in front of it — nothing holds the whole picture.

Phase 4 — Out The Other Side
"My whole life changed
in three days."

This is where the story changes voice. Because this part is mine.

I took the first Elvanse. And within hours — not weeks, not months — everything made sense. The chaos. The addiction. The relapses. The A&E admissions. The ICU. The courts. The shame. The AA meetings that felt wrong. The residential rehab where they took my medication away and called it treatment. All of it.

Not failures of willpower. Not a character defect. What happens when the underlying neurological condition stays unresolved while the system treats only what arrives first.

The dopamine was restored. The executive function returned. For the first time in 35 years, "next week" existed as a felt concept. I could sequence. I could follow through. I could sit with a thought long enough to do something with it.

Within days, I started decorating my flat. I had focus, motivation, drive — not the artificial kind that alcohol gave me, but something clean and real. I started building OTOS. I worked 18-hour days and felt grateful for every one of them.

The therapy started to hold. The CBT landed. The recovery work held — not because the addiction work hadn't been good. It had been excellent. But because the brain finally had what it needed to use it.

I am telling you this not because I want sympathy. I am telling you this because I want you to understand what was possible, all along — if the thread had held between services, if the ADHD had been treated in the context of the addiction, if someone had said earlier: the crisis is visible, but the cause is underneath it.
