Evidence, Sources & Claims Register

Not ahead of the evidence.
Precisely within it.

This page holds the evidence base, policy anchors, source links, caveats and claim discipline behind the OTOS Continuity™ hub.

Every claim is sourced. Every figure is labelled. Every modelling estimate is caveated. OTOS does not make clinical claims. The site separates published evidence, local source material, OTOS modelling estimates and placeholders requiring validation.

01
Claims Discipline

The rule is simple.
Label everything.

Published source
Official guidance, peer-reviewed research, government publication, NICE guidance, NHS publication, or recognised institutional source.
Local source
CPFT, local authority, CGL, CRS, partner or local service data supplied or validated by the relevant organisation.
OTOS modelling estimate
Planning estimate produced by OTOS using published assumptions or local proxy data. Not presented as reported NHS data.
TBC / validate
Useful claim, quote, image, figure or interpretation that must be sourced or confirmed before being used as a live claim.

OTOS surfaces signals. The partner team makes the decision.

Standing governance line: OTOS is non-clinical infrastructure. It does not diagnose, prescribe, replace clinical judgement, merge records or make autonomous clinical decisions.

02
The Core Proposition Being Evidenced

The system often sees addiction first.
But ADHD may be the driver.

The system usually treats addiction as the primary condition. For some people, that is right. For a significant and identifiable group of adults, it is not. The primary condition is ADHD. The drinking or drug use is what arrives in view.

That matters because self-medication can work — until it does not. Alcohol or substances can briefly do what an untreated ADHD brain has been trying to do all along: supply motivation, sustain attention, create focus, cut the noise. The trouble starts when that coping strategy turns into dependency and crisis.

At that point, services quite reasonably respond to the addiction. But if the ADHD remains untouched, the work may not hold.

TBC claim to evidence carefully

“CBT is delivered to a brain that cannot sequence or retain it. Motivation strategies meet an executive function that was never the problem.” This is strong wording but should be backed by a specific ADHD/executive-function/CBT adherence source before being used as a formal evidence claim.

Current policy already points here.

ADHD + Addiction Evidence

This is not a new idea.
It is an underused one.

START Study — addictive disorder outcomes after ADHD diagnosis

Six-month follow-up of patients with ADHD-addiction comorbidity. Reported improvement in addictive disorders in 61.3% of patients. Important comparator, but should be described as observational and requiring confirmation in prospective research.

Evidence Still Needed

Do not overclaim.
Mark the gaps clearly.

Claims needing source confirmation before live use

  • Exact CPFT adult ADHD backlog, medication review and referral figures — attach source document or local confirmation.
  • Exact source for “23–45%” ADHD prevalence range in addiction treatment if used instead of the 21% meta-analysis or 23.1% consensus figure.
  • Exact adult ADHD service restriction / adult-access pause source, if using the “pressure is building” / adult access argument.
  • CBT / executive-function / retention claim source.
  • Turning Point / LSE £4.07 ROI source link and citation chain.
  • Any prison ADHD prevalence / reoffending / relapse comparison.
  • Any “NHS Community Hubs 250” figure — attach the exact source page before making it a hard number.

Evidence is not decoration.
It is the spine.

If a claim cannot be sourced, it stays as a draft note. If a figure is modelled, it is labelled as modelled. If a partner provides wording, it is recorded here so the site remains consistent and defensible.
